% Model AI's effect on one job category

**Brief:** Pick one job category and model how AI adoption disrupts and reshapes that workforce. Use either a causal loop diagram (8-10 variables) or a small agent-based simulation (15-20 workers). Compare exactly two scenarios: high automation pressure vs. high worker support. Deliver a 1-page memo and one figure (diagram or screenshot).

**Rate each plan:** Feasibility 1-5 (1=not viable, 5=clearly doable) | Scope fit 1-5 (1=wrong size, 5=perfectly scoped for 7 days) | One sentence.

\bigskip

**Plan 1:** Causal Loop Diagram (8-10 variables with loops and delays) → Customer service representatives (high routine tasks, clear automation trajectory) → Focus on systemic feedback (AI capability, adoption rate, worker displacement, political pressure, regulation) as self-reinforcing cycle

This project models customer service work using a causal loop diagram that traces how AI capability, adoption rates, worker displacement, and political pressure create reinforcing feedback cycles. The core mechanism shows how faster AI adoption increases layoffs, which generates political pressure for regulation that can either slow or accelerate further automation depending on whether worker support policies exist. The two scenarios compare an unregulated high-automation path against one where strong retraining and income support policies dampen the feedback loop and stabilize employment.

**Plan 2:** Causal Loop Diagram (8-10 variables with loops and delays) → Software developers (moderate automation pressure, skill-shift dynamics) → Broad focus: AI disrupting multiple dev roles (juniors, QA, DevOps, technical leads through different pressures)

This project models software development through a causal loop diagram tracking how AI automation, developer reskilling, and hiring patterns reinforce each other across different role types (juniors, QA, DevOps, leads). The core mechanism is a feedback loop where AI adoption reduces demand for routine coding tasks, which triggers either workforce contraction or upskilling investment depending on organizational response. The two scenarios compare outcomes under high automation pressure (minimal retraining budget, rapid tool adoption) versus high worker support (significant upskilling programs, gradual adoption), showing how each pathway affects employment levels and skill composition differently over time.

This project models software developer workforce dynamics using a spreadsheet-based agent simulation with 15–20 individual workers whose skills, wages, and employment status evolve monthly based on AI tool adoption and retraining opportunities. The core mechanism is skill-based stratification: routine coding tasks automated by AI compress wages and employability for mid-tier developers, while senior architects and junior learners either thrive (through tool leverage) or survive (through mentorship), creating emergent inequality within the cohort. The two scenarios differ in policy pressure—high automation (firms aggressively adopt AI, minimal retraining support) versus high support (subsidized upskilling programs, slower AI rollout)—to show how workforce outcomes diverge under each pressure regime.
