% Model AI's effect on one job category

**Brief:** Pick one job category and model how AI adoption disrupts and reshapes that workforce. Use either a causal loop diagram (8-10 variables) or a small agent-based simulation (15-20 workers). Compare exactly two scenarios: high automation pressure vs. high worker support. Deliver a 1-page memo and one figure (diagram or screenshot).

**Rate each plan:** Feasibility 1-5 (1=not viable, 5=clearly doable) | Scope fit 1-5 (1=wrong size, 5=perfectly scoped for 7 days) | One sentence.

**Plan 1:** Causal Loop Diagram (8-10 variables with loops and delays) → Software developers (code generation and testing) → Process-centric: AI adoption → testing bottleneck relief → project velocity → job role fragmentation (shift from generalist to specialist vs. junior-only roles)

The project models software developer employment using a causal loop diagram with 8–10 variables tracking how AI code generation tools affect job roles in that sector. The core mechanism examines how AI adoption initially accelerates project velocity by automating testing, but then creates a feedback loop where reduced demand for generalist developers drives workforce fragmentation into either specialized senior roles or entry-level-only positions. The two scenarios compare outcomes under high automation pressure (firms rapidly deploy AI with minimal retraining) versus high worker support (companies invest in upskilling and role redesign), showing how each path produces different equilibria in job quality and workforce composition.

This project models how AI adoption affects radiologists using a causal loop diagram that tracks feedback between AI capability, job availability, wages, and retraining flows in the medical imaging labor market. The core mechanism examines how increased automation creates downward pressure on both employment and wages, which then feeds back to influence retraining investment and skill demand—creating reinforcing cycles that either stabilize the workforce or accelerate job loss depending on initial conditions. The two scenarios contrast a "high automation pressure" case where AI tools rapidly displace radiologists with minimal support infrastructure, versus a "high worker support" case where investment in reskilling programs and regulatory constraints on automation create different feedback dynamics and labor market outcomes.

**Plan 3:** Agent-Based Simulation (15-20 workers with states and emergence) → Software developers → Four states (Productive/Struggling/Retraining/Departed) with skill-task mismatch as the core driver

The student will build an agent-based simulation of 15–20 software developers whose career trajectories depend on a skill-task mismatch metric that grows as AI tools shift the demand for certain coding skills. The core mechanism tracks how developers move between four states (Productive, Struggling, Retraining, Departed) based on whether their current skills align with available work, creating emergent workforce churn and specialization patterns. The high automation pressure scenario removes support for retraining and shrinks the job market, while the high worker support scenario funds education programs and maintains hiring, producing different rates of exodus and adaptation in the developer population.

The core mechanic tracks each worker's skill level, job satisfaction, and employment status as AI handles an increasing percentage of incoming requests, with the two scenarios comparing a rapid automation scenario (chatbots take 70% of calls within 6 months) against a gradual transition with retraining support (chatbots reach 50% with upskilling programs that boost worker retention). Emergent outcomes tracked include overall workforce turnover, average tenure, and how many workers transition into different roles versus leaving the organization.
